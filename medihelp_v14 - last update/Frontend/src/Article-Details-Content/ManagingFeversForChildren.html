Introduction

Fever in children is a frequently encountered clinical condition characterized by a temporary elevation in body temperature, typically defined as ≥38.0 °C when measured using reliable methods such as rectal thermometry. It represents a physiological response rather than a disease entity and is most often associated with infectious processes, particularly viral illnesses. Pediatric fever is one of the most common reasons for healthcare consultations worldwide, reflecting both its high prevalence and the level of concern it generates among caregivers. Despite its frequency, fever remains widely misunderstood, often leading to unnecessary anxiety and inappropriate management practices.

From a physiological perspective, fever is a regulated response mediated by the hypothalamus, which adjusts the body’s thermal set point in reaction to pyrogenic stimuli. These stimuli may originate from infectious agents or from endogenous inflammatory mediators produced by the immune system. The resulting elevation in temperature plays a functional role in host الدفاع mechanisms by enhancing immune efficiency, including increased activity of white blood cells and inhibition of pathogen replication. Consequently, fever is generally considered an adaptive and beneficial process rather than a harmful one in most clinical situations.

The management of fever in children requires a balanced and evidence-based approach that prioritizes patient comfort, careful assessment of underlying causes, and recognition of warning signs that may indicate serious illness. Clinical guidelines emphasize that treatment should focus on alleviating discomfort rather than achieving normothermia, and that not all febrile episodes necessitate pharmacological intervention. Proper caregiver education is essential to promote safe practices, reduce misconceptions, and ensure timely medical consultation when indicated.

Definition and Physiology of Fever

Fever results from a regulated increase in the hypothalamic set point triggered by pyrogenic substances. These pyrogens may be exogenous, such as microbial toxins, or endogenous, including cytokines like interleukin-1, interleukin-6, and tumor necrosis factor. These mediators stimulate the synthesis of prostaglandin E2 within the hypothalamus, leading to thermoregulatory adjustments such as vasoconstriction, shivering, and behavioral changes aimed at increasing body temperature.

This process differs from hyperthermia, in which temperature rises without a change in the hypothalamic set point. Fever is generally considered a beneficial adaptive response that enhances immune function and inhibits the growth of certain pathogens

Etiology of Fever in Children

Infectious Causes

  • Viral infections, including respiratory and gastrointestinal viruses
  • Bacterial infections such as pneumonia, urinary tract infections, and otitis media
  • Less commonly, fungal and parasitic infections

Non-Infectious Causes

  • Post-immunization reactions
  • Autoimmune and inflammatory disorders
  • Malignancies such as leukemia
  • Drug-induced fever

Clinical Evaluation

Methods of Temperature Measurement

Method Reliability Clinical Use
Rectal High Infants and young children
Oral Moderate Cooperative older children
Axillary Low Screening purposes
Tympanic Moderate Children older than 6 months
Temporal artery Moderate Rapid, non-invasive use

Accurate measurement is critical for diagnosis and clinical decision-making.

Associated Clinical Features

Evaluation should include assessment of symptoms such as:

  • Altered activity levels (lethargy or irritability)
  • Feeding difficulties
  • Signs of dehydration
  • Rash or skin changes
  • Respiratory distress
  • Neurological symptoms including seizures

Principles of Management

Supportive Care

The primary goal in managing fever is to improve the child’s comfort rather than strictly normalizing body temperature.

  • Maintain adequate hydration through fluids such as water, breast milk, or oral rehydration solutions
  • Encourage rest and minimize physical exertion
  • Dress the child in lightweight clothing
  • Ensure a comfortable ambient environment

Pharmacological Treatment

Medication Pediatric Dose Key Considerations
Paracetamol 10–15 mg/kg every 4–6 hours Preferred first-line agent
Ibuprofen 5–10 mg/kg every 6–8 hours Avoid in dehydration or renal risk

Important precautions include:

  • Avoidance of aspirin due to association with Reye’s syndrome
  • Adherence to weight-based dosing guidelines
  • Avoidance of unnecessary combination or alternating therapy without medical supervision

Non-Pharmacological Measures

Non-drug interventions may complement pharmacological management but should be applied cautiously.

  • Tepid sponging may provide temporary relief but has limited evidence of sustained benefit
  • Cold baths and alcohol rubs are contraindicated due to risk of adverse effects
  • Environmental adjustments should prioritize comfort rather than aggressive cooling

Febrile Seizures

Febrile seizures are convulsions occurring in association with fever in children aged 6 months to 5 years. They are typically benign and self-limiting.

Clinical Features

  • Generalized tonic-clonic movements
  • Short duration, usually less than 5 minutes
  • Occurrence early in the febrile illness

Immediate Management

  • Position the child safely on their side
  • Avoid restraining movements
  • Do not place objects in the mouth
  • Seek medical evaluation after the episode

Indications for Medical Consultation

Medical attention is necessary under the following conditions:

  • Fever in infants younger than 3 months
  • Persistent fever exceeding 3–5 days
  • Signs of severe illness such as difficulty breathing or cyanosis
  • Evidence of dehydration
  • Altered consciousness or persistent irritability
  • Occurrence of seizures

Prevention

Immunization

Routine vaccination plays a critical role in reducing the incidence of febrile illnesses caused by infectious agents.

Hygiene and Public Health Measures

  • Regular hand hygiene
  • Avoidance of contact with infected individuals
  • Maintenance of proper nutrition and sanitation

Common Misconceptions

  • Fever is inherently harmful — in most cases, it is a protective physiological response
  • Higher temperature correlates with disease severity — underlying cause is more important
  • All fevers require medication — treatment depends on discomfort rather than temperature alone

Conclusion

The management of fever in children requires a comprehensive and evidence-based approach that balances the physiological benefits of fever with the need to ensure patient comfort and safety. Appropriate assessment, judicious use of antipyretics, and recognition of warning signs are essential components of effective care. Caregiver education remains fundamental in promoting rational management practices and reducing unnecessary anxiety.
